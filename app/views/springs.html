Comal Springs

Cool, clear Edwards Aquifer water emerges at Comal Springs, as natural artesian pressure forces the water up the faults and fractures in the overlying rocks.

Video 1

Spring flow is dependant upon recharge and conservation. Declining aquifer leves can result in reduced flow, or no flow if water levels drop below the elevation of the spring openings.

Video 2

Spring water provides habitat for aquifer species, water for downstream users, and fresh inflows for the bays and estuaries along the Texas Gulf Coast.

San Marcos Springs

Heading north and east about 20 miles from Comal Springs, you’ll reach San Marcos Springs, the second largest spring system and Texas.

Video 3

Beneath the lake, numerous springs emerge along a common fault line propelling millions of gallons of water to the surface daily.

Deep Springs

EAA technicians and divers from Texas State University work together to take water quality samples from Deep Spring, nearly 30 feet below the surface.

Video 4

Divers insert the pump directly into the spring opening to take the most accurate water quality readings.

Endangered Species

All the Edwards Aquifer Endangered and Threatened species – including Texas Wild Rice seen here – depend on clean fresh water from the aquifer.

Invasive Species

In addition to water quality concerns, exotic invasive species like these elephant ear plants also threaten the habitat of the native species, by competing for food and altering the original riparian areas.

Habitat Conservation

To protect the endangered species long term the EAA, its regional partners, and stakeholders have implemented a comprehensive Habitat Conservation Plan to protect spring flow, water quality, and riparian habitat.
