The Edwards Aquifer — one of the most prolific karst aquifers in the United States — is located right here in south central Texas. Explore and discover the features that make this aquifer one of the most porous and permeable aquifers around.

Seco Sinkhole

For millions of years, water has transformed the landscape of the Balcones Fault Zone in South Central Texas, creating the Edwards Aquifer and the hundreds of caves and karst features within the limestone.

Water Runoff

Beginning along the Southern edge of the Edwards Plateau, the 5,400 square mile Drainage Area funnels water into streams and creeks that flow south toward the Recharge Zone—the outcrop of the Edwards Limestone.

Edwards Aquifer

In the Drainage Area, water emerges from numerous small springs along the Edwards Plateau to create the baseflow for rivers that add recharge to the aquifer. Strong thunderstorms fill the normally dry riverbeds of the Texas Hill Country.

Drainage

As the rainwater mixes with organic compounds in the soil, it forms a diluted weak acid called carbonic acid. Over millions of years this acid has dissolved out some of the calcite minerals that make up the Edwards Aquifer, leaving interconnected pores and cavities, and in some cases large cave systems.

Descent

As the water percolates through the rocks it eventually reaches the water table, or point which the limestone is completely saturated. From here the water moves deeper underground, flowing generally north and east towards discharge points at thousands of water wells, and some of the largest spring systems in Texas.
